# Level 3 — Know More Page (Plain English, Cohort View)

This page is about the *whole group* of applicants who finished Level 3, not one person. Level 3 measures **gut-level associations** — the snap reactions people have before they think. The Level 3 report itself is written by AI for each individual, but the *raw signals* behind every report are clean numbers: how fast people reacted, how often they got it wrong, which words tripped them up. That's what the page should surface across the whole pool.

Today the Know More page is a flat list of personality types. For Level 3 it should answer a different question entirely: **"what unspoken biases and reactions show up across the people I'm hiring?"**

---

## 1. What is this pool's gut-level pattern?

Level 3 tests several themes (career associations, leadership, gender, authority, and so on — whatever modules are turned on for that program). For each theme, the page should show:

- **How strongly this pool leans** — split into four simple buckets: *no bias*, *slight*, *moderate*, *strong*.
- A bar showing the share of candidates in each bucket.
- A one-line plain summary: e.g. *"Most of this pool shows a moderate association between leadership and male names."*

The corporate doesn't need numbers — they need to see, theme by theme, where their pool sits.

## 2. Which themes matter most for *this* pool?

A simple ranked list:

- Themes where the pool shows the strongest collective bias at the top.
- Themes where the pool is neutral at the bottom.

This tells the hiring team where to focus — "for this batch of applicants, the career-vs-family association is the strongest signal; the authority one is flat."

## 3. How trustworthy is the data?

Level 3 is sensitive — if someone clicks randomly or rushes, the result is meaningless. The page should be upfront:

- **How many candidates produced reliable results** vs how many look rushed or noisy.
- A toggle: **"Show only reliable responses"** that updates everything on the page.
- A simple traffic-light for the whole pool's quality.

Without this, the corporate could read bias into noise.

## 4. Which specific words or ideas tripped this pool up?

The test shows words on screen. We can tell which words this *group* consistently slowed down on or got wrong:

- **Top words the pool hesitated on** (per theme).
- **Top words the pool misclassified** (per theme).
- A small word cloud or short list — visual, not a table.

This is where the page goes from "the pool has bias" to "here's exactly which concepts cause it."

## 5. How does the pool compare across slices?

Filters at the top, same data sliced different ways:

- By **job** the candidate applied for
- By **date range**
- By **gender** of the candidate
- By **batch / group**
- By **personality type from Level 1** (e.g. "do the decisive applicants show a different bias than the steady ones?")
- By **reliability** (only trustworthy responses)

When a filter changes, every chart updates. The corporate can ask things like *"do my Sales applicants show stronger leadership bias than my Engineering ones?"* without leaving the page.

## 6. Is this pool uniform or split?

For each theme, show whether the pool is:

- **Uniform** — almost everyone leans the same way, or
- **Split** — half lean strongly one way, half the other.

A uniform pool tells the corporate something about culture. A split pool tells them this group is mixed and individual reports matter more than the average.

## 7. Snap-reaction speed of the pool

A general engagement signal — not theme-specific:

- How quickly the pool reacted on average.
- Whether the pool got faster as they went (learning) or slower (fatigue).
- A flag if a large share of the pool rushed through (likely to be unreliable).

## 8. Drill down to the people

Every theme bar, every bucket, every "strong bias" count should be **clickable**:

- Click → list of candidates in that bucket
- From each candidate row → open their full AI-written Level 3 report
- The AI report stays personal; the page just helps the corporate decide *which* report to actually read.

---

## What the page should *feel* like

- **Theme-by-theme cards**, not one long chart. Each theme is its own story.
- **Plain words, no jargon.** Never use technical terms like "D-score" or "response time in milliseconds" on this page. Use *"slight / moderate / strong"* and *"slower / faster"*.
- **Honest about reliability.** Show data quality before showing conclusions.
- **Filters that act on everything** — comparison is the whole point.
- **Every number clicks through to people.** The current page is a dead end; this one shouldn't be.

---

## What the page should *not* do

- Don't try to summarise the AI's written narrative across the cohort. The prose is per-person; only the structured signals (bias strength, slow words, error words) aggregate cleanly.
- Don't show raw reaction-time numbers. The corporate user does not care about milliseconds.
- Don't lump all themes into one "bias score." Each theme is its own finding.
- Don't show individual report text here — that belongs on the candidate page.

---

## In one sentence

The Know More page for Level 3 should answer **"where does my applicant pool lean unconsciously, which themes matter most, how reliable is what I'm seeing, and which candidates should I read in full?"** — for the whole group, theme by theme, in plain English.
