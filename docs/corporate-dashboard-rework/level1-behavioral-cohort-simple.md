# Level 1 — Know More Page (Plain English, Cohort View)

This page is **not** about one candidate. It is about the *whole group* of applicants the corporate is looking at — everyone who has finished Level 1. The goal is to let the hiring team understand the shape of their applicant pool at a glance.

Today the page just shows a list of personality types with counts. That answers one question: "how many of each type do I have?" — and nothing else. Below is what the page could answer instead.

---

## 1. What does my applicant pool look like overall?

- **How many people finished Level 1** out of everyone who registered.
- **The mix of personality types** — not just the top 4, but the full split, including blended types (like "Driver-Influencer" or "Supporter-Analyst").
- **The most common style** in this pool, and the rarest.
- **Balance check** — is this pool mostly fast-paced/decisive people, or mostly cautious/steady people? Mostly people-focused, or mostly task-focused? One simple "where does my pool lean" picture.

## 2. How trustworthy is the data I'm looking at?

Some candidates rush, some click randomly, some fail the attention checks. The page should be upfront about that:

- **How many candidates gave honest, careful answers** vs how many look rushed or inattentive.
- A single toggle: **"Show only trustworthy responses"** that recalculates everything on the page.
- A simple traffic-light: green / amber / red for the overall pool quality.

Without this, the corporate is making decisions on noisy data.

## 3. Who is in each personality type?

For every personality type shown, the page should tell the corporate:

- **How many people** in the pool have this type.
- **What roles or jobs** these people are applying for most.
- **How careful/honest** this group's answers were (a quick sincerity number).
- **How quickly they finished** the assessment.
- A **"see the list"** button that opens every candidate in this group, so the corporate can act on it — not just read a number.

## 4. How does this pool compare across slices?

The same data, sliced different ways. Simple filters at the top:

- By **job** the candidate applied for
- By **date range** (this week, this month)
- By **gender**
- By **batch / group** (if multiple intake batches)
- By **trustworthiness** (only sincere candidates)

When the filter changes, every chart on the page updates. So the corporate can ask things like "what does my Sales applicant pool look like *this month*?" without leaving the page.

## 5. What did the questions tell us about the pool's behaviour?

The assessment captures more than just the answers — it captures *how* people answered. Things worth showing:

- **Questions the pool kept changing their mind on** — flags areas of indecision across the group.
- **Questions answered too quickly** — flags rushing.
- **Questions where attention checks were failed most** — flags fatigue or disengagement.
- **Average time the pool took** to finish the whole assessment.

These are group-level signals, not individual ones. They tell the hiring team things like "people are getting tired around question 25, we should shorten this for senior roles."

## 6. Funnel — where are people dropping off?

A simple left-to-right strip:

**Registered → Started Level 1 → Finished Level 1**

with the percentage at each step. If 200 people registered but only 60 finished, the corporate needs to see that, not have to calculate it.

## 7. Job fit at a glance

If the corporate has open jobs in the system, show, for each job:

- The personality types most common among applicants for that job
- Whether that matches the ideal profile the corporate (or we) define for that role
- A simple "good fit / partial fit / weak fit" badge for the pool as a whole

This is the bridge from "we have data" to "what should we do about it."

---

## What the page should *feel* like

- **One screen, scannable in 30 seconds.** A hiring manager should learn the shape of their pool without scrolling forever.
- **Every number should be clickable.** Click a personality type → see the people. Click a job filter → see the pool for that job. The current page is a dead end — that's the biggest thing to fix.
- **Honest about quality.** Lead with how trustworthy the data is, not bury it.
- **Comparison-friendly.** A corporate looking at this page is implicitly comparing — this week vs last, Sales vs Engineering, men vs women. Make those comparisons easy with the filter bar.

---

## What we should *not* do

- Don't list traits with just a count and a description. That's what the page does today and it isn't useful.
- Don't show individual candidate detail here — that belongs on the candidate's own profile page.
- Don't show raw scores or technical terms. The corporate user does not care about D / I / S / C as letters; they care about "fast-paced decision makers" vs "steady team players."
- Don't pretend the data is clean when it isn't. If 40 % of the pool answered carelessly, say so.

---

## In one sentence

The Know More page should answer **"what kind of people are in my applicant pool, how reliable is what I'm seeing, and who should I actually talk to next?"** — for the whole group, not one person at a time.
